Improving the way we Shop Online

What’s missing from the online shopping experience? Most of the actual experience, it turns out. Ecommerce is still focused on buying rather than shopping. We’re exploring ways to bridge the gap between physical and digital.

Women make up the overwhelming majority of retail consumers. However, e-commerce sites are designed and built in a tech industry dominated by men. That underlying tension is becoming more apparent and problematic as more and more people shop online. E-commerce sites have largely ignored the preferences of potentially its largest audience-- women.

We decided to talk with women and observe their behaviors in physical stores to uncover what is missing in the current online shopping experience.

Problem #1:

Human habits are ignored

Most e-commerce sites follow same general structure and employ the same navigation and interaction patterns. Products are found through searches or browsed by category. In this structure, there is an underlying assumption that users flow naturally in a linear way from an index page to a product details page to a cart to the checkout.

These patterns are great for targeted searches, but they don't accommodate many other common shopping behaviors. In fact, this process starts to feel more like running queries on a database than like shopping.

Most e-commerce sites are optimized for a utilitarian march toward purchasing.

Current sites feel transactional, stemming from a focus on stakeholder desires. This model largely ignores how customers might envision the experience.

We decided to investigate ways to design a more dynamic and engaging online shopping experience. Below are some ideas for bringing the more social and exploratory aspects of shopping to the online space.

Store design:

  • Presents full ensembles
  • Has visual hierarchy and priority
  • Makes multiple product categories visible
  • Utilizes 3 axes

E-commerce design:

  • Focuses on individual items
  • Sticks to a grid layout
  • Has siloed product categories
  • Utilizes only 1 axis

Problem #2:

The middle step is missing

Our conversations with women revealed that shopping is far more than a means to acquire things. It can be a fun diversion, a way to express one's personal identity or style, or even a way to build trust with close friends and family. Online shopping experiences largely ignore these use cases. In an effort to tighten the sales funnel, they encourage you to go straight from browse to buy without giving you ways to consider your purchases.

By not delivering on expectations, we’re potentially missing out on a powerful consumer group. With that in mind, we focused on three areas where we could bridge the gap.

E-commerce opportunities:

Comparison Shopping

The Problem

In physical stores, shoppers frequently shop not for individual items, but for a collection of items: an outfit, a place setting, or even the ingredients for a meal. But online, it’s difficult to browse multiple product categories simultaneously. Browsing is siloed and constrained to predefined categories, making it difficult to compare dissimilar items. Each search is independent.

Why it matters

The advantages of making comparison shopping easier are two-fold: the potential to convert one initial search into a multi-item purchase and preventing the shopping cart workaround. E-retailers use metrics like cart abandonment to gauge the success of their conversion funnel. But, some of the customers I talked to use the shopping cart like a sandbox.

They throw in all the items they are even remotely considering, so that they can centralize all that information. This is because viewing all that information while browsing the site is difficult. They may never intend to buy more than 10% or 20% of the items they toss into their digital cart.

Current sites feel transactional, which stems from a focus on stakeholder desires. The structure reflects the sales funnel and a functional, utilitarian march towards it. This model lacks the full consideration of how customers might envision the browsing and shopping experience.

Our approach

When we approached the comparison shopping problem, we thought about aways to keep the shopper in the context of her current search, while also revealing a wide array of complementary products. We wanted to create a space where a customer could consider pairings while staying in the browsing and shopping mindset.

Solution

Multiple product categories visible at one time

Physical stores excel at showing a variety of categories at once. You might see a display featuring t-shirts, jeans, and sweaters for instance.

To replicate this online, a panel could emerge suggesting you check out how that item pairs with another product category, sliding in a carousel of items. This approach avoids taking the shopper out of context, and down distracting rabbit holes. This also surfaces up a wider variety of items so there’s a greater chance of matching someone’s personal style.

Solution

A persistant favorites panel

This allows a customer to take items along with them as they shop, much the way you carry items around as you wander the store. Favorites isn’t a new concept, but creating a visible and persistant favorites panel is.

Here, we add a horizontal favorites area. You can drag and drop items in, and build multiple collections or outfits that you can tab through to review.

Conclusion

The key to promoting sales is to give the customer the space and environment to develop an emotional attachment to the products.

Online shopping lacks the fun of real shopping because it’s focused so heavily on getting you to the checkout. Current patterns ignore the way people prefer to shop and treat every product more or less the same. Products are equally competing for the customers attention and the customer isn't guided, making the experience boring and mechanical. By adding features that bridge the gap between analog and digital, we can accommodate common shopping habits.
